Sustainable Fashion Materials: What Brands Are Adopting in 2024

The conversation around sustainable materials has matured considerably since the greenwashing scandals of 2019–2021. In 2024, credibility in this space requires third-party certification, supply-chain transparency, and a shift away from vague language toward measurable claims.

Mycelium leather is the most commercially significant breakthrough of the current cycle. Bolt Threads' Mylo and Ecovative's Forager are now available at meaningful scale, with Stella McCartney and Adidas having completed first-to-market capsules. The material's properties — lightweight, flexible, home-compostable — position it as a credible alternative to bovine leather for accessories and footwear. Cost parity with premium leather is expected by 2026.

Recycled ocean plastic has moved from PR exercise to functional textile. Econyl (regenerated nylon from fishing nets and ocean waste) is now embedded in collections at Speedo, Patagonia, and several fast-fashion mid-tier players. The material performs well in activewear and swimwear categories where durability requirements align with the polymer's properties.

Bio-based polyester derived from corn starch and sugarcane (PLA and PEF variants) is the emerging mid-market play. Unlike conventional polyester, certified bio-based variants can be composted under industrial conditions. Brands targeting ESG commitments with cost constraints should watch this category closely — it is 15–20% more expensive than virgin polyester today but scaling fast.

Linen is experiencing a quiet renaissance. Not a new material, but context-appropriate: low water usage, biodegradable, and increasingly cultivated in Europe with short supply chains. Premium casualwear and tailoring brands are leaning into its wrinkled texture as an aesthetic rather than a flaw — a positioning that aligns with the broader anti-perfection cultural mood.

Key watch: regulatory pressure from the EU's Digital Product Passport (DPP) initiative, rolling out from 2025, will require garments sold in the EU to carry machine-readable data on material composition, durability, and recyclability. Brands not investing in materials traceability infrastructure now will face significant compliance risk.
